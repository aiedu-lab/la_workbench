# Why Linear Algebra?

## Concept

Computers process numbers, not concepts. Linear algebra is the
compact language that turns raw information into numbers a
computer can store, transform, and learn from — and back again
into something we can interpret.

The pattern shows up everywhere data meets a computer:

* An image becomes a grid of pixel values.
* A song becomes a sequence of audio samples.
* A sentence becomes a sequence of numbers (tokens), and each
  token becomes a vector of numbers (an embedding).

Once information is numbers, linear algebra gives us exactly two
things we need: a way to organize many numbers together (vectors
and matrices), and a way to transform them (matrix multiplication,
projections, and the rest of this course). Every modern AI model —
from a small classifier to a large language model — represents its
inputs, parameters, and outputs as vectors and matrices, and does
its work by transforming them.

That is why this course exists: not to teach linear algebra as an
abstract subject, but as the practical language you need to read,
reason about, and eventually build the models discussed in *What
is a Model?* and every session that follows.
